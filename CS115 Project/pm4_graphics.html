How TikTok Has Revolutionized Journalism

Within the past year, TikTok has become a social media giant. With this sudden and massive boost in popularity (especially amongst Gen-Z and millenials) mixed with the intensity of news and media coverage this year, TikTok has become a platform to share news and trending topics in a digestible way to Gen-Z. With TikTok, users are given fifteen seconds to a whole minute to share content. This means the most important parts of news stories are shared so that they can adhere to the fifteen seconds to a minute time rule. Also, with the popularity of TikTok dances amongst Gen-Z, many users on the app use dance moves to share information and news. I want to explore how TikTok differs from other social media apps that have journalistic aspects (Instagram, Snapchat, Twitter, etc.) and TikTok's overall revolutionary affect on the journalism industry.

Brief History of TikTok

Photo Credit: Poynter

TikTok as we know it was created in August 2018 when Chinese tech company ByteDance merged their app, Douyin, with Musicall.ly, an app they bought in late 2017. By October 2018, TikTok surpassed Facebook, Instagram, Snapchat and YouTube in downloads. TikTok grew in popularity throughout 2019, as more media attention surrounded the app and certain users started becoming famous from the app.

Once the globe was put on lockdown due to COVID-19 in March 2020, TikTok became a social media giant. The app was a form of entertainment for many during lockdown and social distancing, especially Gen-Z and millenials. Once this happened, news publications and media companies ran to TikTok in hopes of gaining new readers (particularly Gen-Z readers) through TikTok.

Data Credit: Apptopia

What Separates TikTok from the Rest

What separates TikTok from other social media news sources is the content you make on it. TikTok is driven by short video creation and dances. Because of this, journalistic TikTok users have used popular dance challenges, sounds, and trends on the app in their videos to share news to Gen-Z in a TikTok-esque way. TikTok is like Vine yet more flexlible for journalists and journalism companies. Many news sources who have joined TikTok have felt that it has given them a new younger audience that normally doesn't read their content.

"Unlike text, video media operates on two parallel pathways, conveying explicit information (the kind found in speech or writing) and implicit information (social cues like the TikToker’s clothes and hairstyle, or emotional affect from music) at the same time." This combination, espescially in the format of relatability and humor, catches many people's attention when spreading news.

For example, some doctors on the app used dance challenges to teach viewers the effect COVID-19 has on the body, and what to do to prevent you and your family from getting COVID-19. Video themes included "COVID-19 susceptibility and severity, precaution response efficacy had higher user engagement. Public health agencies should be aware of the opportunity of TikTok in health communication and create audience-centered risk communication to engage and inform community members."

How TikTok's algorithm Affects The Spread of News

Follower count nor engagement rates are taken into consideration with TikTok's algorithm. It is based on what each user likes, interacts with the most, follows, etc. This means it is easier to go viral and become famous on TikTok than other social media apps. Thus news publications, media companies, and journalists can make news content that has a high potential of going viral and being seen by millions, driving traffic to their websites. Also, to have a higher chance of being on the For You page it is important for accounts to post frequently. This means TikTok journalism accounts must come up with at least one post a day to grow their account and reach new readers.

Photo Credit: Giphy

With all of this said, TikTok's algorithm doesn’t come without faults. Many black users have detailed their experiences of being shadow banned and not shown on the For You page. Black users have also found their videos being taken down and shadow banned when calling out racism.

TikTok Users Can Make Headlines

Photo Credit: Pedestrian.TV

TikTok users can go viral themselves and become news stories. In early February 2021, a woman went viral and became a news story after she switched up gorilla glue spray for hairspray. In early March a woman went viral and became a news story after finding a hidden room behind her mirror in her NYC apartment. These are just two examples of the many people who have gone viral on TikTok and became topics in the news due to their popularity. Many find it easy to go viral on TikTok because of its algorithm.

There are also TikTok users such as Charli D'Amelio and Addison Rae who have become newsworthy celebrities in Hollywood. They have paparazzi, celebrity gossip columns written about them, and they always seem to be trending in social media news. Charli D'Amelio's older sister, Dixie, even has her own talk show called, "The Dixie D'Amelio Show" where she interviews her internet friends and allows her audience to see another side of them that is not usually portrayed in the media.

Trends Are Created on TikTok

A lot of trends are popularized on TikTok. Certain clothing, hairstyles, and even quotes from viral TikToks become lingo and go-to topics and sayings for journalists to use in articles.

The Blend of The Opinion Piece and News on TikTok

Photo Credit: Summarizer

On TikTok, alot of news is spread with user's opinions and commentary attached. This has become a huge competitor to written opinion pieces because many would rather listen to a 15 second to a minute video on a topic than read a long written piece. Now many users who don't even identify as journalists will use this format to share their ideas on discourse or popular topics going on in the world. An example of this are teen activists, Jackie James and Sofia Ongele, whose TikToks usually give their opinion on a given topic and additionally share resources on how to help.

What are the cons of using TikTok as a news source? (clickbait, native advertising, false news, bias)

Though popular, verified news publications on TikTok are unlikely to have misinformation issues, regular users can. Also since TikTok is a social media platform having a high follow and engagement rate means influencer deals which can bring about native advertising issues for journalism TikTok accounts.

How are online news sources (Washington Post, NBC, etc.) utilizing TikTok?

NBC News has a mini series called “Stay Tuned” which shares news stories in a digestible way to Gen-Z. It started out on Snapchat, and now has an account on TikTok after TikTok’s surge in popularity in March 2020. Angie Grande, the executive producer of Stay Tuned says, “On Snap, we talk to the viewers, not down to them, and on TikTok it’s even more so.”

With almost 900,000 followers, the Washington Post has had one of the most successful TikTok accounts in the journalism industry. The account is run by Dave Jorgensen, who’s dad jokes and self-deprecating humor have created a loyal fanbase. Typical videos on the Washington Post’s TikTok are skits based on current events. Buzzfeed is passing the baton to teens by putting them in charge. Their TikTok is ran by a group of teen ambassadors who both act and direct/edit/write skits.

For more localized papers, like the Dallas Morning News, a lot of their news is based on local city and state news, current events, customs, jokes, etc. “What’s going on in our community that people can relate to across all platforms?”

The key to reaching Gen-Z is through relatability and humor. News sites also try to use trending sounds, dances/challenges, and hashtags when making videos.
